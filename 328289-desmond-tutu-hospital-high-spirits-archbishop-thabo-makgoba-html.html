Desmond Tutu: Celebrated anti-apartheid icon admitted to hospital

- Desmond Tutu is well known in South Africa for his anti-apartheid campaigns

- The cleric won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1984 through his Desmond Tutu Peace Foundation

- The Archbishop was admitted to hospital on Wednesday, December 4, but was in good spirit

South African Anglican cleric, Demond Tutu, well known for his role as an anti-apartheid and human rights activist, has been admitted to hospital.

According to a statement issued by his foundation, the Nobel Peace Prize laureate had been admitted before for a similar condition in the past years.

“The Archbishop has been hospitalised several times over the past few years for treatment of a similar condition,” the Desmond & Leah Tutu Legacy Foundation said in a statement, without providing further details.

According to his wife, Leah, the clergyman is currently being treated of a stubborn infection.

Archbishop Thabo Makgoba who recently visited the 88-year-old anti-apartheid campaigner said he was in high spirits.

“When I told him that I had told Leah I was coming to scold him out of hospital, he chuckled warmly, which is a good sign. He also said he apologised for making me do so many hospital visits!" said Thabo.

But he assured the nation that Tutu was doing well.

“I went to see him this evening where I found him lucid and engaging. He said he is as good as he can be for an 88-year-old, especially in view of his ill health in childhood," Thabo added.

Tutu, the first black archbishop of Cape Town, was last seen welcoming the World Cup-winning South African Rugby team when they returned to the country in November 2019.

He won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1984 for his non-violent opposition to the apartheid regime.

The cleric once served as the chairman for the Truth and Reconciliation Commission at the direction of then president Nelson Mandela.
