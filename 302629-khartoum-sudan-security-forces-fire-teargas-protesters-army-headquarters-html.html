Khartoum: Sudan security forces fire teargas at protesters outside army headquarters

It was drama in Central Khartoum, Sudan, on Monday, April 8 after security forces lobbed teargas at protestors who had gathered outside the Defence Ministry.

Protests against President Omar al-Bashir were gaining momentum when security forces in pickup trucks intervened as they extensively fired teargas at the protesting citizens.

Citizens have been calling for the resignation of al-Bashir as they faulted the Head of State for the current dwindling economy in the country.

According to witnesses, forces from the army also had to be involved as protesters rallied by their thousands.

Chants of “Sudan is rising the army is rising!” rocked the complex on Sunday at the Defence Ministry which also acts a residence for the Sudan President.

On Monday, with the situation threatening to get out of hand, the government resorted to use of teargas and the army to quell the discontent citizens.

The well-organised protests were set to start on Saturday, April 6, as a way to mark the 1985 uprising that saw the collapse of the administration of then President Jaafar Nimeiri.

At the time, the military successfully managed to eject Nimeiri from office before passing power to an elected government which was also overthrown by al-Bashir in a famous coup in 1989.

TUKO.co.ke has since learnt the protests were triggered by the government’s move to triple the price of bread back in December 2018.

What started as a simple protest against price-hiking quickly morphed into a full blown protest against al-Bashir’s government, with a large section of citizens demanding his exit from office.

Meanwhile, the Sudan leader has maintained he would not step down and urged his opponents to seek power through constitutional means.

More than 30 people have been reported dead since the protests began in December, with six others confirmed to have died in the latest wave of chaos since April 6.

Aside from the killings, activists, Opposition leaders and journalists have also been jailed by the National Intelligence and Security Service which is very feared in the country.

As far as the economy is concerned, Bashir conceded that citizen’s concerns were legitimate, although he imposed an emergency rule in February because he felt the protests were not justified at all.

His term is set to end in 2020 although it remains to be seen whether he will actually complete it especially since he is being accused of mismanaging the economy.
