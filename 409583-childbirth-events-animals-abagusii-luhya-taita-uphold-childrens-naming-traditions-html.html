Naming traditions and patterns form the very intricate practices in most African communities to date.

Celebrations were often held in many communities to welcome newborns into the families. Naming formed part of these special occasions.

Among the Maragolis in the Luhya community, like other communities, children were often named after their ancestors and even those still alive.

Esther Anini told TUKO.co.ke that on receiving the news of newborns, male elders and older women would delve into the name searching and giving exercise.

Unlike other communities, Anini said that baby's physical appearance baby was also a determinant factor in their name.

“Before the elders, the baby’s grandmother would keenly check for any birthmark on the baby. The birthmark would task them to rekindle one of their ancestors who had a similar birthmark during their days,” she said.

In cases of no birthmark, Anini said that the fingers, nails, ears, toes, or even skin complexion were crosschecked if they resemble one of their gone clan members.

She added that some children adamantly refused the names given to them by refusing to breastfeed and crying persistently.

In some instances, babies died frequently, and this raised the alarm on the fate of their parents’ dynasty.

Animal names were given to children who got born after their predecessors died.

“Some parents would struggle to get children and only do so after a long time. When these parents would get the babies after a long wait the babies would, unfortunately, pass on at birth or while toddlers.

“These babies associated to bad spirits that would die and go back to the womb to be born again and then die in a bid to torture their parents,” Phoebe Murembo, an elderly Maragoli woman narrated.

To save such children, Maragoli community members believed that a scary name would be given to him or her to scare away the spirit of death.

Such scary names included Rusimba/Risimba, which means a lion, Manani [ogre], Monyo (red ants), Riaga (Aardvark), Mbiti (hyena), and Gundu (a beast).

Notably, some names were determined by the season or environment in which the baby was born.

For instance, Jane Afandi explained that children born during the rainy season were called Ambura/Avura, which means rain.

Those born during the harvest season were called Nekesa/Wekesa, while those born during times of hunger were called Nanjala for females while Wanjala for male equal in Bukusu, Kabras, and Tachoni sub-tribes of the Luhya.

Children who were born during the colonial period were called Ngereso to mean Muingereza in Kiswahili which is an equivalent of a white settler in English.

Other names included; Vusaka (bush), Anzira/Kinanzira (road), Kibwoni (potato farm), Mugera (river).

These names were given to children who came as a surprise when their mothers were busy on their errands like fetching firewood for Vusaka, walking on the road for Kinanzira, working in a potato farm for Kibwoni, and fetching water from the river for Mugera.

Abagusii community

In the Abagusii community, it is common for children to be named after their ancestors.

Members of the community also told TUKO.co.ke that they also name children after animals and birds.

The emphasis here is usually the character of the animals or could be seasons in which the animals were dominant.

Zablon Mato Ocharo, a Kisii elder, further explained to TUKO.co.ke that if a mother keeps losing her newborns, it is believed that human spirits have denied their existence and will therefore opt to become part of the animal kingdom.

Some are named after elephants, buffalos, leopards, and hyenas, and birds.

"Someone is called Nyang’au which means hyena, Nyanjoku was named after an elephant, and Ongera after buffalo. Teffaki is a Kisii name of an eagle," Ocharo narrated.

Taita community

The Taita community also has its unique way of naming newborns. In the community, the firstborn child is named after the paternal grandmother or grandfather.

John Mwakisha from Kishushe from Wundanyi told TUKO.co.ke that the second-born child, on the other hand, is named after the maternal grandfather or grandmother.

“The third born is named after the paternal grandparents depending on the gender. The fourth born is named after the maternal grandparents depending also on gender,” Mwakisha explained.

It is not in parents’ power to choose the gender of their children, as this is purely a biological happening. This means there are instances where parents can bear either boys or girls only.

Mwakisha explained that in such an instance when a child is named after grandparents from both maternal and paternal grandparents, names would be sourced from other members of the family.

“In some occasions, names for the newborns would be sourced from a beloved deceased sister or brother. Names of delinquent people would, however, not be given to children as it was feared the vices would be transferred to the newborns,” Mwakisha added.

Violent and truant individuals’ names would hardly be passed to newborns.
