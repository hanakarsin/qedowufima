Matiangi enlists students in war on corruption

- After ordering that no more satellite higher learning campuses will not be registered, Education CS Fred Matiangi has now devised a way on how his Ministry will be monitoring funds allocated to public schools

- Each student will be given a unique personal identification number that will track how resources are spent as well as their education progress

Education Cabinet Secretary Fred Matiangi has come up with  way to curb misappropriation of funds sent to public schools by his ministry.

According to the Standard, Matiangi accused some school heads of conspiring to steal government issued resources to the tune of millions annually.

To curb this, the ministry is planning to issue every student in the public school system a personal unique identification number to be used from nursery to university.

According to statistics from the ministry, KSh 28 billion is remitted to public secondary schools annually, and another KSh 14 billion to primary schools.

Each primary school child receives KSh 1,420 annually while those in secondary receive KSh 12,687.

In 2014, schools recorded that 9.95 million children had enrolled in schools against the actual 9.55 from a census conducted.

It was also found that in the same year, high schools quoted having enrolled 2.33 million students while they had in fact enrolled 2.31 million.

Sources close to the matter told the Standard that it was estimated that school heads could be handling up to KSh 300 billion a year.

This is all aside from the KSh 116.6 billion collected from parents annually. The ministry was worried that they could be losing money through inflated enrollment figures from the learning institutions.

If the numbers submitted to the schools are to be computed, the amount paid for nonexistent students amounts to KSh 568 million in primary schools and KSh 253 million in secondary.

This latest directive comes in the wake of Matiangis order that there would be no more registration of new satellite higher learning institutions in Kenya.

This is because the Ministry believes that these campuses water down the quality of education and produce incompetent professionals to the market.

Kisii University was among the institutions whose 10 campuses were shut down a month ago over poor quality education.
